Special Relativity

Olin Robus

24/03/14

0

I love science fiction, and I love the idea of space travel and space exploration. But as I age my ability to suspend disbelief declines, and I lose ever more patience with unrealistic depictions of traversing the genuinely incomprehensible distances that space travel requires.

But it doesn’t have to be like this! I’m happy to suspend disbelief if some attempt has been made to reckon with the consequences of a fictional FTL solution. This essay is an attempt to, for myself, get clear on all the various aspects of Special Relativity one must grapple with in order to have a happily plausible FTL. If, in the process, we verge into philosophical territory and find ourselves meditating on the intersection of epistemology, metaphysics, the philosophy of science, and the philosophy of language, well, that’s my fault.

1 The Basics

Special Relativity is a theory of how to measure distances between events. There are a couple of things to say about this already—first, to say that it is a theory is to simply say that it is a mathematical model (or rather a set of such models) that have (a) specific correlates to the experienced world, and (b) a procedure for how to take those correlates and produce results that are themselves possibly correlated to our experience in the appropriate ways. While this may not be a usual way to speak of a scientific theory, we can see it maps on to more typical accounts—the correlates are typically spoken about as ‘initial’ or ‘boundary’ conditions for a theory, while the procedure is more or less the collection of ‘laws’ and the constellation of practices and norms around their application and calculation. Finally, the results of operating on the correlates via the procedure are what are usually understood as ‘predictions’. So, in more familiar language, we might gloss the notion of a scientific theory as “The application of natural laws on initial conditions to produce predictions.”

Now, just to be clear, this is not how science works, nor is it an accurate picture of how scientists see themselves. Indeed, it obviously falls short in many sciences where initial conditions, laws, and predictions are not and clearly defined as they are in—say—mathematical physics. My point in characterizing Special Relativity (SR) this way is to emphasize what it looks like ‘from the outside’ as it were. As we get deeper into learning about SR, it will become easier and easier to think and speak as if we were thinking and speaking about the world, when we are in fact speaking about a theory which cannot be an adequate description of reality, simply because it is not an adequate description of our experience (here philosophers would say the theory is not ‘empirically adequate’). Nevertheless, it’s still a worthwhile theory to study, since it does serve as an incredibly adequate theory for our experiences within a certain domain.

As I said above, SR is a theory about how to measure distances between events. Now that I’ve gotten off my high-horse about what a theory is, let’s look at the other parts of this statement. In particular, what stands in most need of explanation is what we mean by ‘distance’ and ‘event’. You may have heard that SR isn’t about space and time, but rather spacetime. And that’s not wrong—Einstein and others before him recognized that measurements of distance, especially in the realm of the physical sciences, could not afford to decouple the notions of spatial and temporal distance. This becomes clearest when attempting to measure things which are either very big, or moving very quickly. But an important consequence of this was the recognition that we cannot measure distances between objects, or ‘things’, but instead must measure events. ‘Event’ is a pedestrian word that has been elevated to a privileged status in SR and the physical sciences more generally. An event for SR (and for us) is analogous to a point in Euclidean geometry—a dimensionless geometric primitive. But with SR being specified in a 4-dimensional vector space, one of whose dimensions is ‘time’, the basic idea of a point was extended to the notion of a dimensionless, instantaneous, ‘happening’. Thus, just like \mathbb{R}^2 can be thought of as constructed out of the points p \in {\mathbb{R}\times\mathbb{R}}, the spacetime of SR is constructed out of the union of all ‘events’.
